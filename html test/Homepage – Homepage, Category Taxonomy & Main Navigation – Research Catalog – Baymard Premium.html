Homepage

This topic covers the primary objectives of the homepage, and the opportunities it can provide to new and repeat users.

Although many users, including first-time visitors, will typically land directly at an intermediary category page or a specific product page through direct links (e.g., links shared on social media), promotions (ads and PPC), or through organic search results, the homepage is still a vital aspect of an e-commerce store.

Executive Summary

The homepage remains the “front door” to the many users who still begin their browsing experience here, but it also acts as an anchor and a place to refer back to throughout the shopping session.

Therefore, it’s critical that the homepage makes it clear to users — new as well as repeat — how to pursue the three primary product finding paths: category navigation, search, and curated paths (wizards, inspiration, new arrivals, etc.). For new visitors who are unfamiliar with your site or brand, the homepage is also an opportunity to shape the user’s perception and answer vital questions such as: what type of site did I land on, what can I do here, and what should I expect to find?

In practice, serving all these diverse user needs on a single page is no easy task. It requires a delicate balance between the different elements on the homepage so that category navigation doesn’t fall second to the often visually striking homepage content (e.g. featured products, campaigns and promotions). Yet at the same time, category navigation shouldn’t be so attention-grabbing that they draw focus away from the primary content on other pages throughout the site. With these conditions met, the homepage should simultaneously inspire users to explore, convey the site’s brand values and product range, and promote scope selection and product finders — all without causing visual overload, which tends to lead to banner blindness.

In short: the homepage is a balancing act. The challenge is to resolve the inherent tension between core site-wide elements and the homepage content (featured products, campaigns, etc.) and functions (brand value, user stimulation, portraying product range, etc.).

Guidelines

Feature a Broad Range of Product Types on the Homepage #237

Many first-time users rely on the homepage content to interpret the type of site they’ve landed on and the diversity of products available. Misinterpretations during this first assessment can lead to abandonments.

Be Cautious if Displaying Ads on the Homepage #240

Ads on the homepage are generally perceived negatively, often set a poor first impression, and on mobile sites in particular can cause overview, distraction, and interaction issues.

Ensure Carousels Respond Appropriately to User Interactions and Feature Prominent Controls #242

If a carousel is difficult to interact with, users may dismiss it or overlook valuable content.

Avoid Autorotating Carousels on Mobile Homepages #957

Users will fail to discover vital features when they are only placed in an autorotating carousel and have a hard time interacting with it.

If Providing a Scoped Path on Mobile Homepages, Include the Full Scope in the Link Text #958

Users on the homepage sometimes fail to notice when they’re tapping a link to a narrow scope, which can lead to a misunderstanding of what products are actually available.

Always Use Bespoke Imagery and Design to Present the Site’s Brand #238

First-time users may underestimate the quality and appeal of products if imagery is merely functional and plain.

Feature a Representative Sample of Products on the Homepage for DTC, Small Catalog Sites #1708

For direct-to-consumer and other small catalog sites, failing to communicate the site’s product range can cause users to misinterpret the site and delay users’ ability to understand the scope of the product catalog.

Always Highlight Core Product and Brand Features on the Homepage for Niche DTC sites #1707

Users are quick to evaluate not only a site’s product catalog, but also the brand’s policies, value proposition, and trustworthiness, from the homepage, and lack of clarity towards any of these concerns can result in premature abandonment.

Consider Highlighting Recent Orders on the Homepage #3051

Users often need quick access to open and recently delivered orders.

Consider Offering “Thematic” or “Guided” Product Browsing #249

Trying to find inspiration for a product to buy can be very difficult when only using category navigation or search.

Make It Clear Where Hit Areas in Visual Elements Lead #264

Users can miss shortcuts to desired destinations or take unintended detours when links in visual elements are unclear.

Always Link the Site Logo to the Homepage #257

If users can’t get to the homepage quickly from anywhere on the site, some will find it more troublesome to restart product finding after encountering an issue.

Consider Promoting and Embedding Wizards Directly on the Homepage #247

Although wizards can be very helpful for users that need help in choosing products, many may never see them.

Consider Using IP Geotargeting to Select Country and Language #239

If setting country and language preferences isn’t straightforward, product finding and checkout could be needlessly cumbersome.

Consider Including the Main Navigation Categories Directly on the Homepage #236

In some industries, category browsing generally performs better than other product-finding strategies, yet users often aren’t sufficiently encouraged to adopt the strategy on the homepage.

Benchmark Analysis

Desktop Web

Despite being the best-performing topic within the theme of Homepage & Category, the average site had a “mediocre” performance with 44% of sites obtaining “mediocre” or worse performance scores and 27% of sites with “poor” or worse performances. Interestingly, the Homepage topic is the only area where more than half (56%) of sites have “decent” or better UX performances.

Generally, sites did extremely well representing their product types on their Homepages, with only 11% displaying less than the suggested minimum 40% product type representation (#237). In addition, 62% of the sites showcased their main navigation product categories directly on their homepage (#236). Supplementing that, 84% of sites implemented some kind of ‘Thematic’ or ‘Guided’ product browsing on their homepage (#249), indicating a variety of inspirational paths for users to begin exploring the sites’ product catalogs.

Beyond that, some of the most commonly observed Homepage issues include the following:

  • More than 32% of sites featured some kind of distracting ‘ad-looking’ content within the top part of their homepages potentially resulting in soured first impressions and unnecessary distractions (#240).
  • 30% of the sites that used carousels had issues that made it difficult to use and control the carousel rotation (#242).
  • 27% of sites missed out on including key product or brand details on the homepage (#1707).

For inspiration, look to Crate & Barrel, IKEA, and Williams Sonoma for their state-of-the-art implementations of this topic.

In addition, the Page Designs tool contains a collection of 490+ design examples of the Homepage.

Mobile Web

Within the topic of Mobile Homepage, the average site performance is almost identical to its desktop counterpart, with 58% of sites performing “decently” or better. It is also the highest-performing subarea within the Mobile Homepage & Category Navigation area, with most sites featuring a broad range of product types and categories, alongside thematic and guided product browsing options. That said, three of the most commonly observed Mobile Homepage issues are:

  • 33% of sites set a poor first impression by placing ads in primary areas of the mobile homepage, causing overview, distraction, and interaction issues (#240).

  • 65% of sites failed to provide the full scope in the link text of highlighted paths. This can confuse new users who may mistakenly perceive the product catalog to be limited or small when clicking on one of these highlighted paths and arriving at an overly narrow scope (#958).

  • 35% of sites had interaction and usability issues with their autorotating or manual carousels, ranging from the inability to support swiping gestures to text within the carousel being difficult to read, and more (#957).

For inspiration, see L.L. Bean, Nike, and Urban Outfitters for their “state-of-the-art” Homepage implementations, or visit the Page Designs section where we have collected 480+ design examples of the Mobile Homepage.
